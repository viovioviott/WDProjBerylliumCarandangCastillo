Andromeda Constellation

The Andromeda Constellation was already a well known constellation among the ancient peoples such as the Greeks. They named it after their mythological princess named Andromeda, and it was also among one of the 48 constellations recorded by Ptolemy in his “The Almagest. The myth of Andromeda was of a beautiful princess of her father, King Cepheus. When King Cepheus’ kingdom was in danger of being destroyed by the god Poseidon, her parents had attempted to sacrifice their daughter in order to save their kingdom. Fortunately, the prince Perseus spotted the princess in danger on his flying horse: the Pegasus, and by the nick of luck had saved her from imminent danger, living happily ever after. In the Northern Hemisphere, Andromeda, seen as a figure with open arms can be observed from August to February, meanwhile in the Southern Hemisphere, it would be visible instead from October to December.


Alpha Andromeda

There only exist a few stars within Andromeda’s boundaries with an apparent magnitude higher than 3.00. Among these, Alpheratz or α Andromeda, is the brightest “star” in the constellation, with it actually being composed of two other stars which form a “binary system”. The bigger and smaller stars are 200x and 10x brighter than our Sun respectively!

Mirach

Forming the left hip of the figure of princess Andromeda, scientists calculate this star to be 1900 times brighter than the Sun, yet it appears to be dimmer than the previously mentioned Alpha Andromeda binary system, due to it being at a distance 200 light years away from Earth.

Andromeda Galaxy

Also found in the Andromeda Constellation is the famous Andromeda Galaxy, our galaxy's closest other major galaxy! It can be found at the right side of the star Mirach.
